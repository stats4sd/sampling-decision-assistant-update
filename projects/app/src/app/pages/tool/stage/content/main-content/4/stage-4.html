To design a sampling plan, it is important to define the reporting requirements or goals. Depending on how you intend to report results, you may or may not have to consider your total sample size and method of selection differently.

The first question is to decide if you wish to divide your population to produce disaggregated estimates. If you need to give different figures for separate groups within your population, then you actually need an estimate for each one.

Now think about the characteristics of the population you want to disaggregate by. The UNHCR Policy on Age, Gender and Diversity is a good starting point. Other variables you might want to disaggregate by can be geographic such as camps or districts, or relate to the characteristics of persons of concern like the country of origin. Make sure the disaggregation variable refers to the final sampling units or a higher level: For example, if your sampling unit are households, you can disaggregate them by district (a higher level), or by gender of the head of household (which is a household-level characteristic). You cannot disaggregate them by education, gender or other characteristics of individual persons unless you can make this a household-level characteristics like for heads of household.

Keep in mind that adding too many disaggregation characteristics leads to large required sample sizes. Try to focus on the one or two disaggregation characteristics that are most important for your study. Often strata are formed to define reporting requirements and in those cases this tool makes no difference between strata and reporting requirements. Sometimes, you may need to divide your population into strata to improve the precision of your estimates, but you have no requirement for estimates disaggregated by those strata.

Provide a name or reference for the estimate. This should be the same as, or an abbreviation of your indicator
